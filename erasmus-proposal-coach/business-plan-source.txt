Business Plan
Erasmus+ Application Support Business
For Rob Bangert and Kata Kadek
Prepared: 30 April 2026

1. Executive Summary
Rob Bangert and Kata Kadek can build a strong, scalable business around the Erasmus+ Proposal Coach by positioning themselves not as traditional paid consultants, but as expert guides whose knowledge is embedded in a paid application platform.

Core principle:
Clients do not pay primarily for hours of consultancy. They pay for access to the app, the structured workflow, the proposal intelligence, the guide search, the reusable partner library, and a limited expert success layer that helps them use the tool well.

This model is stronger than classic consultancy because:
• it is scalable
• it reduces dependence on billable hours
• it creates recurring revenue
• it turns expert knowledge into a product
• it allows support to be standardised

The best business model is a product-led advisory business:
• paid app access is the main revenue engine
• onboarding, workshops, and review moments are included only in controlled formats
• Rob and Kata remain visible as trusted experts, but the app does the repetitive work

2. Market Opportunity
The Erasmus+ market is large and active enough to support a focused niche business.

Relevant official context:
• Erasmus+ had an annual budget of EUR 4.7 billion in 2024
• in 2024 the programme supported more than 34,400 projects and involved over 85,600 organisations
• almost 1.5 million people took part in Erasmus+ mobility in 2024
• the 2026 Erasmus+ call was published on 12 November 2025

This means there is a continuous market of schools, youth organisations, cultural institutions, municipalities, and small foundations that need help writing better proposals.

The strongest customer segment is not the already highly professional Brussels-style grant office. The strongest segment is:
• smaller and medium-sized organisations
• first-time or second-time applicants
• organisations with limited internal writing capacity
• cultural and educational institutions that have ideas but need structure
• youth and arts organisations that struggle with deadlines, budget logic, and Erasmus wording

5. Business Model
The right business model is institutional subscription plus controlled advisory layers.

Recommended revenue model

Layer 1: App licence revenue
This is the main business.

Suggested pricing structure:
• Solo or micro-organisation licence: EUR 290 to EUR 490 per year
• Small institution licence: EUR 900 to EUR 1,500 per year
• Multi-user institutional licence: EUR 2,500 to EUR 6,000 per year
• Network or umbrella licence: EUR 7,500+ per year

What is included in the licence:
• access to the app
• future app updates
• proposal examples
• partner library and project storage
• programme guide search
• finance helper
• AI-ready workflow
• limited email support

Layer 2: Onboarding revenue or onboarding included in higher tiers
Suggested formats:
• 90-minute onboarding session
• 2-hour team setup workshop
• partner-library and workflow configuration

This can be:
• included in premium licences
or
• sold as a one-time setup fee

Suggested fee if sold separately:
• EUR 350 to EUR 950

Layer 3: Review moments, not open-ended consultancy
Important rule:
Do not offer unlimited free advisory work, because then the app licence becomes hidden consultancy.

Instead, create fixed expert formats:
• one proposal review round
• one finance review round
• one partner-role review round
• one deadline sprint session

This can be:
• included in premium tiers in limited quantity
or
• sold as add-ons

Suggested pricing for add-ons:
• proposal review sprint: EUR 450 to EUR 750
• full institutional application review: EUR 900 to EUR 1,500
• team workshop: EUR 950 to EUR 1,800

Key principle:
The customer pays for the platform. Expert time is structured, limited, and productised.

6. Service Boundary
This is crucial for profitability.

Rob and Kata should not become unpaid ghostwriters.

Recommended boundary:
• you help organisations think, structure, improve, and review
• the organisation itself remains responsible for the final submission
• support is delivered through the app, templates, webinars, and fixed-format review moments
• custom writing is the exception, not the default

A useful rule for every sale:
"The app does the drafting work. We help your team use it well."

9. Best Way To Promote The Business
Best overall strategy:
Deadline-led authority marketing.

This is the best promotion model because Erasmus+ demand is seasonal and deadline-driven. Organisations do not look for support every day. They look when:
• a new call opens
• they decide to apply
• deadlines approach
• they realise they need structure

So the best way to promote the business is not broad generic branding first.
The best route is:
1. publish practical authority content tied to specific Erasmus actions
2. run short public webinars before application deadlines
3. offer a low-friction trial or demo of the app
4. convert institutions into paid app users
5. upsell onboarding and review sprints

This is much stronger than trying to sell consultancy directly.

15. Risk Analysis
Risk 1: Too much unpaid support
Response:
Use strict support boundaries and fixed advisory formats.

Risk 2: Clients expect ghostwriting
Response:
Position the app as guided drafting, not done-for-you writing.

Risk 3: Seasonal sales pattern
Response:
Use deadline campaigns and build annual licences, not one-off rush sales only.

Risk 4: Too broad a customer base
Response:
Start with culture, youth, and education organisations where your credibility is strongest.

Risk 5: Tool seen as "just another form helper"
Response:
Lead with expertise, examples, reviews, workflow, and official guide intelligence, not only software features.

16. What Is Allowed Within The Erasmus+ Regulatory Framework
This section is important because your business model sits close to proposal preparation and project implementation support.

Important principle:
Erasmus+ projects are submitted and implemented by participating organisations. The organisation remains the applicant and, if selected, the beneficiary.

What is generally allowed
Based on the Programme Guide and related financial provisions, the following is generally compatible with Erasmus+ rules:
• selling access to an app that helps organisations prepare applications
• providing training, onboarding, IT support, budgeting support, and structured drafting support
• providing limited technical or specialised services, if a beneficiary later uses your service during project implementation and the service is non-core, necessary, documented, and procured correctly
• supporting institutions before submission with proposal structure, workflow, and review tools
• providing expert review or editing support, as long as the applicant organisation remains the real author, owner, and responsible party for the proposal

What the official rules clearly allow
The official Erasmus+ guidance explicitly says that limited subcontracting of services is allowed, provided it does not cover core activities on which the achievement of the objectives of the action directly depends.
The guidance also says that tasks performed by the coordinator under the grant agreement cannot, as a rule, be subcontracted.
For procurement of goods, works, or services, beneficiaries must ensure best value for money, avoid conflicts of interest, and keep documentation for audit.

What this means for your business
This means your app business is strongest and safest when it is positioned as:
• a preparation and drafting support tool
• a technical and methodological support service
• a non-core support layer around the application process

What is risky or should be avoided
You should avoid designing the business in ways that make it look as if:
• you are the real applicant instead of the institution
• you are taking over the institution's core project coordination role
• the funded project can only function if your external company runs its core content
• your costs are hidden, not documented, or not transparently described
• the same cost is funded twice
• procurement or conflict-of-interest rules are bypassed

Practical "not allowed or high-risk" situations
These models are not advisable:
• selling a hidden ghostwriting service that replaces the organisation's own project responsibility
• asking funded beneficiaries to present your app cost as a vague internal management cost without clear procurement logic
• structuring your role so that core Erasmus+ project design, delivery, or coordinator tasks are effectively outsourced to your company
• making success-fee arrangements that are not transparent or do not fit beneficiary procurement and cost rules

Safest business model under Erasmus+
The safest model is:
• pre-award: institutions buy access to the app and use it to prepare applications
• pre-award or post-award: institutions may buy clearly defined non-core services such as onboarding, training, technical setup, or one review sprint
• post-award: if your app or support is used during implementation, it must be framed as a non-core technical or support service, not as outsourced coordinator responsibility

Recommended wording for your offer
"The Erasmus+ Proposal Coach is a drafting, workflow, and technical support platform. Institutions remain fully responsible for the content, submission, implementation, and compliance of their Erasmus+ projects."

Recommended compliance rules for Rob and Kata
• never present yourselves as the applicant
• never guarantee funding success
• do not position the app as a replacement for the beneficiary's core responsibilities
• if an institution wants to pay from project funds after award, require them to check internal procurement and eligibility rules first
• keep invoices and service descriptions specific: app licence, onboarding, training, review session, technical support
• separate pre-award support from post-award implementation support in your commercial offer
